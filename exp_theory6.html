Proximate analysis of coal


1.0 TITLE: Proximate analysis of coal


2.0 PRIOR CONCEPT: Combustion, calorific value, types and composition of coal and its uses.


3.0 NEW CONCEPT:


PROPOSITION 1:

The composition of coal varies with source and age of the coal. In order to decide the quality, rank and the price, two types of analysis are carried out-
(i) Proximate analysis
(ii) Ultimate analysis
Proximate analysis of coal deals with the determination of percentage of moisture, volatile matter. ash and fixed carbon whereas ultimate analysis is determination of %C, %N, %S and % O.


PROPOSITION 2:

Proximate analysis of coal is carried out by pyrolysis of coal in crucible in oven. The method is "proximate" because the results of these analysis may vary and depend on the procedure adopted for the analysis like temperature of heating, duration of heating material of crucible used etc.

PROPOSITION 3:

Fixed Carbon is the useful Carbon present in the coal and directly relates to the quality of coal. It can be obtained by subtracting sum of % moisture, % Volatile matter and % ash from 100.


4.0 Information Input:
Significance of Proximate analysis:

(a) Moisture
i)Moisture is evaporated during burning of coal and in doing so, it takes some part of the liberated heat. Thus, it reduces calorific value.
ii) It quenches the fire in the furnace.br
iii) It increases the ignition temperature of the fuel.
iv) It adds to handling, storage and transportation cost by its weight.
Hence, lower the moisture, better is the quality of coal.

b) Volatile matter
i) Volatile matter is not originally present in the coal, but it is formed and escaped during combustion process.
It consists of combustible gases such as H₂, CO, hydrocarbons like CH, and noncombustible gases such as CO₂ and N₂
ii) A high volatile matter containing coal burns with long flame. It requires larger furnace volume iii) Coal with higher volatile matter burns with smoke and low heating value.
Hence, lesser the % of volatile matter, better the quality of coal.

c) Ash
i) Ash is useless, non-combustible matter containing SiO2, Al2O3, CaO, MgO, FeO etc. It reduces calorific value.
ii) It increases the transportation, handling and storage cost on account of its weight.
iii) It involves additional cost for its disposal.
iv) It causes early corrosion and wear of furnace walls and burning apparatus.
Hence, lesser the % of ash, better the quality of coal.

d) Fixed Carbon
1) Out of total carbon content in the coal, part of carbon may participate in the formation of volatile matter and may escape.
Higher the percentage of fixed carbon, higher will be the calorific value.
It consists of combustible gases such as H₂, CO, hydrocarbons like CH, and noncombustible gases such as CO₂ and N₂
Hence, higher the % fixed carbon, better will be the quality of coal.


5.0 Learning Objectives:
5.1 Intellectual Skills:
1. To understand the concept of Proximate and ultimate analysis.
2. To understand the adverse impact of moisture, volatile matter and ash on the quality of coal and ultimately on its cost.
3. To understand the determination of % of actual useful fixed Carbon of the coal.

5.2 Motorskills:
1. Ability to handle fragile crucible and other glassware.
2. Ability to work with oven (temp<= 350 deg * C ) and muffle furnace (temp. ≤1000℃)


6.0 Stepwise Procedure:

6.1 Part A - Moisture Content
1) Take known quantity of powdered coal sample in previously weighed silica crucible.
2) Heat the sample in an electric oven at about 100 to 110°C for about an hour.
3) Take out the crucible, cool it in desiccators and weigh it.
4) Repeat the same procedure of heating and weighing the crucible till constant weight is obtained.
Note down the constant weight.

6.2 Part B - Volatile Matter
1) Take known quantity of powdered coal sample in previously weighed silica crucible.
2) Heat the crucible in a muffle furnace at 950+20°C for exactly 7 minutes.
3) After heating take out the crucible and allow it to cool and weigh it.
6.3 Part C - Ash
1) Take residual coal which is moisture free and volatile matter free (ie from part B).
2) Weigh it and take it in a silica crucible.
3) Ignite it openly for half an hour at 700-800°C
4) Cool it and weigh it. It is weight of ash.
